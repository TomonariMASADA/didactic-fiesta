Brandom speaks insistently of substitution, but with respect to nonlogical vocabulary, one needs to speak not only of substitution but also of elimination.

Consider A and B as nonlogical vocabulary. When using A and B is the same as using A alone — that is, when adding B is indifferent to A (our concept corresponding to Brandom's notion of invariance is precisely this indifference) — B is "implied" by A. The elimination of nonlogical vocabulary allows us to account for the "implication" relations that hold among nonlogical vocabularies. Whether implicit or explicit, this is an "implication" relation that belongs in a lower layer than the implication relations between propositions. One can say that propositions are what make these lower-layer "implication" relations explicit.

The nonlogical vocabulary referred to here (this is Brandom's term) corresponds, in terms of the discussion of semiosis conducted so far, to the signifier. To restate this in the language of semiosis: when a given signifier is indifferent for the signifiers that constitute its context, that signifier is eliminatable. Conversely, if a given signifier is not indifferent for the signifiers that constitute its context, then that signifier means something else — something further.

(Note: "A is indifferent for B" means the same thing as "B is indifferent to A".)
